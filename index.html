With the lockdown came new challenges, and due to health concerns, Canadians were no longer able to connect with their loved ones as they had before.

The Pandemic and Emotional Wellbeing

The ramifications of the COVID-19 pandemic will likely still be felt for years to come.

It clear that the pandemic took a toll on the public's mental health.

In a 2024 study by the Government of Canada, it was found that:

Social isolation as a result of lockdowns and other societal changes during the COVID-19 pandemic led to a significant increase in mental health disorders such as anxiety, depression and post-traumatic stress disorder, resulting in higher outpatient and emergency department mental health–related visits for youths.

—Assessing the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on the mental health–related hospitalization rate of youth in Canada

During a time when social isolation was required for our health and safety, people found other ways to foster connection:

Our smartphones and other devices became essential tools for staying connected.

The Pandemic and Our Smartphones

Studies have shown that the COVID-19 pandemic led to a widespread increase in daily average screen time.

In a survey conducted by Statistics Canada, nearly 80% of parents reported seeing their children engage in more recreational screen time, as compared to pre-pandemic levels.

Furthermore, there is evidence that this overall increase in screen time has followed the public (and especially children) out of the pandemic.

We are using our phones more than we ever have before.

Problematic Smartphone Use

A University of Toronto study found that one-third of people show high levels of smartphone addiction.

Younger users, especially women, are at higher risk.

The Link Between Screen Time and Mental Health

The CDC reports that teens spending 4+ hours daily on screens are more likely to experience anxiety and depression.

This is due to:

  • Dopamine-driven social media cycles, fostering addictive habits.
  • Social comparison, lowering self-esteem.
  • Disrupted sleep, due to screen exposure before bed.

Did you know?

Teens in cities are more likely to have 4+ hours of daily screen time than those in rural areas.

Social Media and Emotional Impact

A growing body of research suggests that social media may be taking a toll on mental health.

A systematic review by Keles found a strong link between social networking site use and higher levels of depression, anxiety, and psychological distress.

While this doesn’t prove that social media causes these issues, the correlation is too significant to ignore—especially for adolescents, who are the most vulnerable.

Compared to younger children, teenagers show a much stronger connection between social media use and mental health struggles, raising serious concerns about its long-term effects.
